Case Study

Wizly

A study app that uses a wizard companion to add an element of fun and motivation.

Timeline

1 month

Role

Solo — Designer

Tools

Figma

Overview

Wizly is a self-guided UI/UX concept for a study app that combines notes, flashcards, study guides, and a pomodoro timer — all guided by a wizard companion who reacts emotionally to how you're doing. Users can upload class materials and generate flashcards and study guides, organize content by course, track assignments, and study to ambient background music. The goal was to make studying feel less like a chore and more like something you'd actually want to come back to.

The Problem

I learn best when I'm being quizzed by someone else. Studying with friends always felt more effective and more engaging than working through a flashcard app alone. But that's not always possible, and most solo study tools feel mechanical: you get an answer right or wrong, and the app moves on without acknowledgment. I wanted to design something that brought the social, encouraging energy of studying with a friend into a solo experience.

Why a Wizard?

I wanted to create a sense of fun and immersion, almost like studying at a wizarding school. More than the aesthetic though, the wizard serves a functional purpose: he gives the studying experience emotional presence. When you get an answer right, he celebrates. When you get one wrong, he encourages you to try again. I deliberately made him speak in first person so he feels like his own entity rather than a notification. He's my way of blending productivity with playfulness.

The Process

Low-Fidelity

At this stage, I wasn't thinking about color or visual style at all, I focused entirely on structure and flow. I wanted to map out the information architecture first: how content would be organized by course, how moving between notes and flashcards would work, what would happen when a user saved a file or generated a new set of cards. Getting the logic right before the visuals felt important for an app with this many moving parts.

Mid-Fidelity

This is where the visual identity started to take shape. I went back and forth extensively on the purple palette — trying different shades to find ones that felt right alongside the wizard's design, which I had sketched out by this point. I also brought in placeholder fonts that matched the concept's personality. This was the stage where the project first started to feel like what I had imagined it could be.

High-Fidelity

In the final pass, I replaced placeholder text with real dialogue and made refinements to every screen featuring the wizard. His color was a constant source of indecision, and I kept returning to it to get it right. I also focused on making his responses feel more genuinely encouraging, particularly after a wrong answer, so the emotional tone stayed warm and supportive rather than neutral.

Colors

The palette is built around shades of purple — a color associated with mystery and the otherworldly — accented with bright yellow and mint green. The contrast between the deep purples and those brighter pops gives the app a slightly eerie, slightly playful quality, somewhere between a spellbook and Halloween.

Fonts

I chose Fontdiner Swanky, Henny Penny, Modern Antigua, and Crimson Text for their hand-crafted, slightly archaic character — the kind of typography you'd expect to find in a book of spells. Together they reinforce the wizarding world atmosphere without feeling costume-y.

Feedback & Reflection

Sharing early prototypes with a few classmates, the response to the wizard companion was genuinely positive. Wizly started from a real frustration of my own: studying alone never felt as effective as studying with someone else, and I couldn't find an app that came close to replicating that energy. Working through three stages of fidelity taught me to think purely in terms of logic and structure before getting attached to any visual direction, and the hardest part was committing to the wizard's color palette. I second-guessed it constantly, but I'm glad I stayed with it because it captures the playful, slightly eerie feeling I was going for.
