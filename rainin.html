Case Study

Rainin Vero — UX Flow Mapping

Mapping the on-device screen flows for Rainin's Vero electronic pipette line as a design intern.

Timeline

Summer 2025

Role

Design Intern

Tools

Adobe XD

Overview

As a design intern at Mettler Toledo Rainin, I worked on the on-device screen flows for the Vero line of electronic pipettes — laboratory instruments used by scientists to measure and transfer liquid with precision. Vero has since launched publicly, and the flows I worked on are part of the interface scientists interact with every time they use the device. Because the specifics of this project are covered under an NDA, I'm sharing it here at a high level, focused on the type of UX problem I worked on and how I approached it.

The Problem

Vero's on-device interface is built around a small screen and a joystick — there's no keyboard, no mouse, and no touchscreen to lean on. Every interaction a scientist needs, from adjusting volume to navigating settings, has to happen through a constrained set of physical inputs. My work focused on mapping out how users would move through these screens: what each step in a flow needed to show, what the joystick should do at each point, and how someone could complete a task quickly without getting lost in menus. This is a fundamentally different kind of UX problem than designing for a phone or web app — every assumption about touch, scroll, or click has to be rethought for a single-button, single-joystick interaction model.

My Role

I was the design intern supporting a cross-functional team that included engineering and product. My main responsibility was building out detailed flowcharts in Adobe XD that mapped how users would navigate between screens — covering everything from primary actions like adjusting pipetting volume to secondary flows like settings and protocol management. These flowcharts served as a shared reference point between design, engineering, and product, helping the team align on exactly how each interaction should behave before it was built.

Constraints:

  • Designing for a tile-based, joystick-only interface with no touchscreen or keyboard input
  • Working within an established visual and interaction system already in place for the product line
  • Collaborating closely with engineering to ensure flows were technically feasible to build

The Process

Flow mapping for a hardware interface like this meant thinking in very literal, step-by-step terms — every joystick movement (up, down, left, right, click) had to lead somewhere intentional. I worked closely with engineering and product to understand technical constraints early, then mapped out flows showing how a user would move from one screen to the next to complete common tasks, like adjusting a pipetting volume or saving a protocol. Each flowchart went through multiple rounds of review with the team to make sure the logic was both intuitive for the end user and feasible to implement.

A meaningful part of this process was learning to communicate UX decisions to a cross-functional audience. Engineers needed precise, unambiguous logic; product needed to see how a flow served the broader user need. Mapping the same flow in a way that satisfied both was one of the more challenging — and valuable — parts of the internship.

Outcome

The Vero line has since been publicly released, and the screen flows I contributed to are part of the on-device experience scientists use today. While I can't share the specific flowcharts or internal documentation due to the NDA, seeing a real, manufactured product reach the market with work I contributed to was an incredibly rewarding outcome for an internship.

Reflection

This internship taught me how different UX design is when the "screen" is a few square inches on a physical device with no touch input. It pushed me to think in much more literal, constrained terms than I was used to from app or web design, and it gave me real experience collaborating with engineering and product, not just other designers. I also learned how to communicate design logic clearly to people with very different priorities than mine, which is a skill I expect to rely on in any cross-functional team I work with going forward.
